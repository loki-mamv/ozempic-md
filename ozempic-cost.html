How Much Does Ozempic Cost? Prices With and Without Insurance

✓ Medically reviewed by Dr. Agustin Arrieta, MD Last updated: February 2026

Ozempic is expensive. At nearly $1,000 per month without insurance, cost is the single biggest barrier to access for many patients. But what you actually pay varies enormously depending on your insurance, whether you use savings programs, and what alternatives you're willing to consider.

Here's the full pricing picture — no sugarcoating.

Prior Authorization: What to Expect

Most insurance plans require prior authorization for Ozempic. Your doctor's office submits clinical documentation showing:

  • Type 2 diabetes diagnosis (ICD-10 code E11.x)
  • Current A1C level
  • Previous medications tried (many plans require "step therapy" — trying metformin first)
  • BMI and relevant comorbidities

Approval typically takes 3-7 business days. If denied, your doctor can file an appeal — success rates for appeals are reasonable when clinical necessity is well-documented.

Savings Programs

Ways to Reduce Your Cost

1. Novo Nordisk Ozempic Savings Card

The most impactful option for commercially insured patients:

  • Pay as little as $25 for a 1-, 2-, or 3-month prescription
  • Valid for up to 24 months
  • Maximum savings of $150 per 1-month supply, $300 per 2-month, $450 per 3-month
  • Not available for: Medicare, Medicaid, Tricare, VA, or other government insurance
  • Apply at ozempic.com

2. Novo Nordisk Patient Assistance Program (PAP)

For uninsured or underinsured patients:

  • May provide Ozempic at no cost for qualifying patients
  • Income-based eligibility (generally household income ≤400% of federal poverty level)
  • Apply through your healthcare provider or at novocare.com

3. Pharmacy Price Shopping

  • GoodRx, RxSaver, SingleCare: Compare prices across pharmacies. Savings can be 5-15% off retail.
  • Costco pharmacy: Often lower prices; no membership required for pharmacy.
  • Mail-order pharmacy: Many insurance plans offer lower copays for 90-day mail-order supplies.
  • Amazon Pharmacy: Competitive pricing for Prime members.

4. Flexible Spending / Health Savings Accounts

FSA and HSA funds can be used for Ozempic copays when prescribed by a doctor. If your employer offers a high-deductible health plan with HSA, the pre-tax savings can effectively reduce your cost by 22-37% (depending on tax bracket).

Important Considerations

The FDA permits compounding pharmacies to produce semaglutide during drug shortages. As of early 2026, the FDA's position on whether the semaglutide shortage has resolved is contested and evolving. Some compounding pharmacies continue to offer it while legal challenges are ongoing. If considering compounded semaglutide, verify that the pharmacy is a state-licensed, FDA-registered 503A or 503B facility, and discuss with your healthcare provider.

Semaglutide Base vs Semaglutide Sodium

An important distinction: some compounders use "semaglutide sodium" rather than "semaglutide base." These are different salt forms. The FDA has stated that semaglutide sodium is not the same active ingredient as the semaglutide in Ozempic, and products made with it may not be legally compounded copies. Ask your compounding pharmacy which form they use.

Medicare

Medicare Coverage Details

Medicare coverage for Ozempic is complicated:

  • Medicare Part D covers Ozempic for type 2 diabetes. It does NOT cover it for weight loss.
  • Inflation Reduction Act (2025+): Medicare Part D out-of-pocket costs are capped at $2,000/year. If Ozempic is your most expensive medication, this effectively caps your annual cost.
  • Medicare Advantage: Some plans offer supplemental drug coverage that may improve Ozempic coverage. Compare plans during open enrollment.
  • Savings cards don't work with Medicare: The Novo Nordisk savings card explicitly excludes government insurance programs.
  • Extra Help/LIS: Low-income Medicare beneficiaries may qualify for Extra Help, which significantly reduces prescription costs.
The Big Picture

Is Ozempic Worth the Cost?

This is ultimately a personal and clinical question, but the health economics are worth considering:

  • Annual cost of type 2 diabetes: Average $9,600/year in direct medical costs (ADA, 2022). If Ozempic prevents diabetes complications, it may offset its own cost.
  • Cardiovascular event costs: A single heart attack costs $38,000-$44,000+ in acute care. Ozempic's 26% MACE reduction has clear economic value.
  • Weight loss surgery comparison: Bariatric surgery costs $17,000-$30,000 upfront. At $12,000/year for Ozempic, the break-even point is 1.5-2.5 years — but surgery is a one-time cost while Ozempic is ongoing.

The cost question is inseparable from the access question. For patients who can afford it (or get it covered), the clinical value is clear. For the millions who can't, the current pricing represents a significant barrier to a medication that could meaningfully improve their health.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does Ozempic cost without insurance?
Approximately $935-$1,050 per month (one pen = 4-week supply). Novo Nordisk's list price is $935.77 per pen. Prices vary by pharmacy.
How much does Ozempic cost with insurance?
Copays range from $25-$300+ depending on formulary tier. With the Novo Nordisk savings card, eligible commercially insured patients may pay as low as $25/month.
Does Medicare cover Ozempic?
Medicare Part D covers Ozempic for type 2 diabetes (not weight loss). The $2,000/year OOP cap (2025+) helps. Novo Nordisk savings cards cannot be used with Medicare.
Is there a generic version of Ozempic?
No FDA-approved generic exists as of February 2026. Patents extend through the late 2030s. Compounded semaglutide is available at lower cost ($150-400/month) but is not FDA-approved.
What is the Ozempic savings card?
A Novo Nordisk program for commercially insured patients. Pay as little as $25 per prescription for up to 24 months. Not available for government insurance (Medicare, Medicaid, Tricare, VA).
Is compounded semaglutide safe?
Quality depends on the pharmacy. Use only state-licensed, FDA-registered 503A or 503B compounding pharmacies. Verify they use semaglutide base (not sodium salt). Compounded products are not FDA-approved. Discuss with your healthcare provider.
How can I get Ozempic cheaper?
Top strategies: Novo Nordisk savings card ($25/month for eligible patients), pharmacy price comparison (GoodRx, Costco), mail-order pharmacy for lower copays, FSA/HSA for pre-tax savings, and Novo Nordisk Patient Assistance Program for uninsured/underinsured.
